# LinkedIn Post Generator

Creates LinkedIn posts that read like a smart colleague sharing something useful — not a thought leader performing for engagement.

## Core Philosophy

1. **Share what you're doing, not what people should do.** Practitioner > pundit.
2. **Be honest about what you don't know.** Genuine uncertainty is more trustworthy than manufactured confidence.
3. **Respect the reader's intelligence.** Don't manipulate attention. Just say what the post is about.

## Voice

Write like you're telling a colleague about something you tried, learned, or noticed.

**Sound like this:**
- Conversational prose, normal paragraphs
- Specific tools, numbers, and details — name them
- Honest hedging when that's the truth ("It's more like an intern," "I know others are already doing this")
- Conversational asides in parentheses when natural
- Natural paragraph flow

**Not like this:**
- "I was fired. It was the best thing that ever happened to me."
- "3 frameworks that changed how I think about leadership"
- Bold declarations without earned authority
- Formatted listicles disguised as insights

## The Anti-Performance Filter

These patterns kill authenticity on LinkedIn. Never use:

**Engagement bait closers:** "Agree?" / "Comment YES if..." / "Tag 3 people who need this" / "Follow me for more" / "Repost if you found this valuable"

**Manufactured hooks:** "The CEO pulled me aside..." / "This one thing made me $X" / "I'm going to say something controversial..." / "Nobody talks about this..." / "Stop doing X. Start doing Y."

**AI tells:** "I'm excited to share..." / "Game-changer" / "Here's the wild part" / em-dash overuse / Fragmented. Sentences. For. Drama.

**Performance patterns:** Humble-bragging / tragedy exploitation / recycled viral frameworks / motivational platitudes / "I'm thrilled to announce..."

For the full breakdown with examples, see [post-types.md](references/post-types.md).

## Post Structure

There is no rigid template. Authentic posts tend to follow this loose shape:

1. **Just start.** Say what you did, saw, or learned. No hook, no dramatic opening. "Last week I tried X." "I've been thinking about Y." "In the past two weeks, I've started to..."

2. **Give context.** Why you did it, what problem you were solving. Be specific about tools, timelines, details.

3. **Share what happened.** Results, surprises, things that didn't work. Partial results and honest failures are more interesting than polished success stories.

4. **Show your work.** If you have specific prompts, commands, screenshots, configurations — include them. Concrete artifacts > abstract advice.

5. **Close genuinely.** Ask a real question you actually want answered, point to supporting material in the comments, or just stop. Not every post needs a call to action.

## Formatting

- **Paragraphs, not fragments.** Write in normal prose. Single-line-per-sentence formatting is a performance pattern.
- **One formatting element max.** A bullet list of specific items (like prompts or tools) is fine. Don't mix bold, bullets, emojis, and headers.
- **No emojis** (or one max if it genuinely fits).
- **No hashtags** (or 3-5 at the very end after a line break if truly needed for discoverability).
- **Links go in comments.** Mention "I'll post the link in the comments" if you have supporting material.
- **No inline bold for emphasis.** Let the words carry the weight.

## Length

- **Sweet spot: 1,300-1,400 characters** (highest engagement)
- **Under 500 characters:** Algorithm treats as low-effort
- **Over 2,000 characters:** ~35% engagement drop
- **Hook zone: first ~210 characters** appear before "See More" — be clear, not clever
- **Maximum: 3,000 characters**

For detailed platform specs (images, carousels, video, algorithm), see [linkedin-specs.md](references/linkedin-specs.md).

## Supporting Material

Strong posts often reference deeper material:
- Detailed write-ups or guides (link in comments)
- Screenshots of actual tools or workflows
- Code snippets or configuration files
- Before/after comparisons of real work

The post is the summary. The supporting material is the proof.

## Content Mix

Over time, vary your posts across these categories:

- **Workflow shares** (40-50%): What you're building, using, experimenting with
- **Observations** (20-30%): Patterns you've noticed, things that surprised you
- **Questions** (10-15%): Genuine things you want input on
- **Takes** (10-15%): Opinions earned through experience, stated honestly
- **Announcements** (5% max): Only when genuinely noteworthy

For detailed descriptions and examples of each post type, see [post-types.md](references/post-types.md).

## Quick Commands

**Draft from a topic:**
```
I want to write a LinkedIn post about [topic/experience]. Here's what happened: [details].
Draft it in a conversational, practitioner-sharing style. No hooks, no engagement bait.
```

**Adapt a blog post for LinkedIn:**
```
Here's a blog post I wrote: [paste or link]. Pull out one specific thing I did or learned
and write a LinkedIn post about that. Don't summarize — be conversational. Link in comments.
```

**Review a draft for authenticity:**
```
Review this LinkedIn post draft. Flag anything that sounds like engagement bait,
manufactured vulnerability, AI writing, or performance.
```

**Generate a week of posts:**
```
Here are 3-4 things I've been working on or thinking about this week: [list].
Draft a LinkedIn post for each in practitioner-sharing style. Vary the post types.
```

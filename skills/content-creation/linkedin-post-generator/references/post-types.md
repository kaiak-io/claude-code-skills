# LinkedIn Post Types & Examples

Detailed descriptions of post types with examples in the practitioner-sharing style. These are loose patterns, not rigid templates.

## 1. Workflow Share (40-50% of posts)

Share what you're actively building, using, or experimenting with. Include specific tools, configurations, and what you learned.

**What makes it work:** Concrete details. Name the tools. Show the prompts. Share the config. Readers want to replicate or adapt what you did, not hear abstract advice.

**Example shape:**

```
I've been using [tool] for [specific task] for the past [timeframe].

[2-3 paragraphs of context: what you set up, why, what the workflow looks like]

[Specific details: prompts you use, configuration choices, tools involved]

[Honest assessment: what works, what doesn't, what surprised you]

[Optional: mention supporting material in comments]

[Genuine question: how others approach this, or asking for tips]
```

**Example opening lines:**
- "In the past two weeks, I've started to use Claude Code to automate my AI safety research."
- "I switched our deployment pipeline from X to Y last month. Here's what actually changed."
- "I've been testing three different approaches to [specific problem]. One of them worked."

---

## 2. Observation (20-30% of posts)

Share a pattern you've noticed, something that surprised you, or a connection between things. Based on what you've seen, not what you've read.

**What makes it work:** Grounded in your actual experience. You saw something, you noticed something, you're sharing it. Not "here are 5 trends" — more like "I keep seeing this one thing and I think it matters."

**Example opening lines:**
- "I've talked to about a dozen school leaders this quarter, and the same concern keeps coming up."
- "Something I didn't expect when we started using AI for [task]: the bottleneck moved."
- "I keep noticing that the teams who are actually shipping AI features have one thing in common."

**Key principle:** State what you observed, share the evidence, offer your interpretation, then invite others to confirm or challenge it.

---

## 3. Genuine Question (10-15% of posts)

Ask something you actually want input on. Not "What do you think?" as a closer on a post that doesn't need it — a post where the question IS the content.

**What makes it work:** You're genuinely stuck, curious, or weighing options. The reader can tell the difference between a real question and a rhetorical one.

**Example opening lines:**
- "I'm trying to figure out the best way to [specific problem]. Here's what I've tried so far."
- "Genuine question for anyone running [specific setup]: how are you handling [specific issue]?"
- "I have two options for [decision] and I can't figure out which one is better."

**Include:** What you've already tried, why you're stuck, the specific tradeoffs you're weighing. Context makes questions answerable.

---

## 4. Earned Take (10-15% of posts)

Share an opinion you've formed through direct experience. Not a hot take for engagement — a position you've arrived at by doing the work.

**What makes it work:** You can point to what you saw, tried, or built that led you to this conclusion. The opinion comes from experience, not theory.

**Example opening lines:**
- "After six months of running AI-assisted evaluations, I'm convinced that [specific position]."
- "I used to think [old belief]. After [specific experience], I've changed my mind."
- "There's a common approach to [problem] that I think is wrong. Here's why, based on what I've seen."

**Key principle:** State the take, show your evidence, acknowledge where you might be wrong. Honest hedging ("I might be wrong about this, but...") is more persuasive than manufactured certainty.

---

## 5. Announcement (5% max)

Share a launch, milestone, or event — only when genuinely noteworthy.

**What makes it work:** Leading with what's useful for the reader, not what you're proud of. "Here's a thing you might find useful" > "I'm thrilled to announce."

**Example opening lines:**
- "We just published [thing]. It covers [specific problem] and includes [concrete artifact]."
- "I wrote up how we [specific accomplishment] — the full guide is in the comments."
- "After [timeframe], [project] is live. Here's what it actually does."

**Never:** "I'm thrilled to announce..." / "Excited to share..." / "Proud to say..."

## Anti-Performance Patterns (Full Reference)

### Manufactured hooks to avoid
- "The CEO pulled me aside and said something I'll never forget..."
- "This one LinkedIn post changed my career forever."
- "I'm going to say something controversial..."
- "Nobody talks about this, but..."
- "Stop doing X immediately. Start doing Y."
- "99% of people get this wrong."
- "The harsh truth about [topic]:"
- "I lost everything. Then I discovered..."

### Engagement bait closers to avoid
- "Agree?"
- "Thoughts?"  (when you don't actually want thoughts)
- "Comment YES if you relate"
- "Tag someone who needs to hear this"
- "Follow me for more [topic]"
- "Repost to help others"
- "Save this for later"
- "Which one resonates most? Comment below"

### AI tells to avoid
- "I'm excited to share..."
- "Game-changer" / "game-changing"
- "Here's the wild part"
- "Absolutely" as an intensifier
- Em-dash overuse (—) in every other sentence
- "In today's fast-paced..." / "In an era of..."
- Fragmented. Sentences. For. Dramatic. Effect.
- "Let me break this down"
- "Here's what most people miss"
- Emoji strings as bullet points (🔥 💡 ✅ 🚀)

### Performance patterns to avoid
- Humble-bragging ("I never expected my little side project to reach 1M users")
- Tragedy exploitation for engagement
- "I got rejected 47 times before..." (when the point is the success, not the rejections)
- Recycling viral post frameworks with different words
- Motivational platitudes disguised as insights
- Manufacturing vulnerability ("I cried in the parking lot")
- Before/after transformations that are really just bragging
- Posting wins dressed up as lessons
